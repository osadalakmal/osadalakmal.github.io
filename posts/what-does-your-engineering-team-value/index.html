What Determines Your Engineering Organization’s Culture

All Happy Families

The Anna Karenina Principle states that “All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way.” This concept, coined by Leo Tolstoy, suggests that unhappy families possess unique traits that prevent them from being happy, while happy families avoid these traits, resulting in their happiness.

This principle is evident in many real-world examples. One notable instance is from the book “Guns, Germs, and Steel” by Jared Diamond. Diamond argues that the rise of the modern world was due to firearms and disease spread. He applies the Anna Karenina principle to domesticated animals, stating that several properties must hold for a species to be domesticated:

  1. It should be easy to feed.
  2. It should grow up quickly.
  3. It should breed in captivity.
  4. It should be easy to handle.
  5. It should be easy to train.

Any species lacking even one of these attributes cannot be domesticated. Similarly, all domesticated animals share these attributes.

From my experience, effective engineering teams also possess a few key attributes. While there is no single right way to run a software engineering team, there are various wrong ways. Two key attributes stand out for me:

  1. The overall engineering organization’s reporting structure.
  2. The values that get the most airtime in the organization.

The Overall Engineering Organization’s Reporting Structure

I have been fortunate to work for several different companies across various industries and engineering organizations. I have also interacted with people from diverse companies, including startups, enterprises, contracting houses, product companies, and both established and newly formed organizations.

One of the most crucial differences among these organizations is their reporting structure. The formal organizational structure is less important than the informal one. Observing senior executive interactions reveals who holds whom accountable. This dynamic often depends on whether there is explicit reporting of one discipline to another. Even in organizations that ostensibly treat various disciplines equally, senior-level interactions reveal the true accountability structure.

Everyone Reports to Engineering

This is common in engineering shops focused on consulting and long-term contracting. The business revolves around creating engineering talent and marketing it. Everyone from delivery teams and business analysts to marketing and sales reports to their engineering counterparts. The CTO holds the most power at the executive level, reflecting throughout the ranks.

This has interesting consequences:

  • Engineering becomes multidisciplinary out of necessity. Engineers must understand and direct other disciplines, learning their vocabulary and ways of working. They frequently interface with other teams, learning to direct them and achieve desired outcomes from delivery, sales, and other teams.
  • Engineering feats, particularly those that are sellable, are celebrated. The business relies on creating engineering outputs, whether software or tools/processes that can be sold.

Everyone Reports to Delivery

In some organizations, everyone reports to delivery or project management teams. The delivery teams report to a senior executive in charge of software services, common in software services businesses. Delivery teams are in charge because their performance directly affects company revenue. This dynamic has its own characteristics:

  • Engineering is measured by ease of delivery, resource consumption, and on-time completion. Engineering quality takes a backseat to delivery speed, leading to conservative tech usage and slow transitions away from legacy tech.
  • Output-focused organizations often cut corners and use temporary fixes. Bugs are resolved with standard operating procedures (SOPs) rather than true fixes. Change requests (CRs) are needed for any alterations.
  • These organizations are process-heavy to minimize risk. Documentation and adherence to processes are crucial to prevent disruptions and shield the engineering organization from blame.

Everyone Reports to the Core Business

This is typical for product-centered organizations. Here, engineering reports to the product team, merging the technical and product roadmaps. Technical decisions are driven by business impact, and engineering quality correlates with product development vision and discipline.

  • Engineering is measured by business value delivery. Good organizations use OKR-based systems to evaluate impact on product agility and consumer value. Poor measurement methods, like counting features, can demotivate teams.
  • Extreme feature delivery focus turns teams into feature factories, leading to short-term thinking, hasty coding, and lack of system ownership.
  • Processes are minimal and kept light. Product teams often dictate deadlines, leading to faster but less consistent output compared to engineering-led organizations.

What Gets Valued in the Engineering Organization

This aspect is often overlooked but crucial. Even during interviews, pay attention to what interviewers consider significant achievements. These values can shape organizational performance. None of these values are inherently good or bad; balance is key to avoid cultural blind spots.

Amount or Impact of Product Features Developed

Organizations valuing product features delivered tend to be lean and delivery-driven. Engineering quality is secondary to delivery speed.

Depth of Knowledge and Experience in the Product

Some organizations value deep product knowledge due to poor knowledge sharing and high attrition. Arcane product knowledge becomes valuable, leading to cautious changes and reliance on experienced individuals.

Engineering Prowess and Innovativeness

Large tech-focused businesses often value engineering prowess and innovation. This can lead to engineers pursuing trendy technologies for vanity projects, necessitating vigilance against such tendencies.

Final Thoughts

This post is based on my experiences and discussions with industry peers. While it generalizes anecdotal evidence, I believe human nature and incentives often produce the outcomes described. If you find any inaccuracies or have different experiences, please reach out to me on LinkedIn.
